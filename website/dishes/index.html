Moldova's Cuisine

A taste of Eastern Europe's best-kept culinary secret

Home Traditional Dishes Drinks & Wine

From slow-simmered soups to wood-fire grilled meats, Moldovan cooking rewards patience. Most iconic dishes have roots in the peasant kitchen — simple ingredients transformed by long cooking times and careful seasoning.

Soups & Starters

Zeamă (Chicken Noodle Soup)

The soul of Moldovan home cooking. A whole chicken is simmered with root vegetables until the broth runs deep gold, then sharpened with borș — a fermented wheat brine that gives zeamă its signature sourness. Fine egg noodles and fresh dill finish the bowl. Every family has a slightly different recipe, usually guarded fiercely.

Răcituri (Meat Jelly)

Pork trotters, ears, and knuckles are cooked for hours until the collagen-rich broth sets into a clear, firm jelly. Eaten cold, scattered with garlic and parsley, alongside horseradish or mustard. A winter staple and a test of a cook's patience.

Main Dishes

Sarmale (Stuffed Cabbage Rolls)

Minced pork is mixed with rice, onion, dill, and black pepper, then rolled tightly into leaves of pickled cabbage (varză murată). The rolls are layered with smoked ribs and simmered in tomato sauce for two to three hours. No Christmas or Easter table is complete without them.

Mămăligă cu Brânză și Smântână

Thick cornmeal porridge served hot with crumbled sheep's cheese (brânză de oaie) and a generous spoonful of sour cream. Sometimes a fried egg is added on top. It is the defining Moldovan comfort food, eaten at breakfast, lunch, or late at night.

Friptură (Roast Pork)

Pork shoulder marinated in garlic, thyme, and white wine, then roasted slowly in a clay pot until falling tender. Served with pickles and mămăligă. At village feasts, the clay pots go directly from the wood-fired oven to the table.

Pastries & Breads

Plăcinte

Thin rounds of dough fried or baked with a variety of fillings: salty cottage cheese with dill, mashed potato with caramelised onion, sweet cherries or apples in summer. The fried version puffs up at the edges and crisps to a light gold. Eaten warm, ideally with a glass of cold kefir.

Cozonac (Festive Sweet Bread)

An enriched, yeasted loaf swirled with walnut and cocoa filling, or sometimes poppy seeds soaked in warm milk. Baked for Easter and Christmas, cozonac takes most of a day to make properly — the dough must be kneaded until it pulls away from the bowl in smooth, elastic sheets.

Must try

Tocană de Porc

Pork and onion stew slow-cooked with tomatoes and peppers. Rich, paprika-red, and deeply savoury.

Must try

Ghiveci

A vegetable stew of courgette, aubergine, peppers, and tomatoes, cooked down until nearly jammy. A late-summer staple.

Must try

Fasole cu Afumătură

White beans braised with smoked pork and paprika. Warming, smoky, and filling — peasant food at its finest.
