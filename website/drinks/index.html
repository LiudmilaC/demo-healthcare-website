Moldova's Cuisine

A taste of Eastern Europe's best-kept culinary secret

Home Traditional Dishes Drinks & Wine

Moldova is one of the world's most wine-dense nations. The vine has been cultivated here since ancient times, and today winemaking is as much a cultural identity as an industry. But there is plenty to drink beyond wine: fruit brandies, fermented dairy, and herbal teas round out the Moldovan table.

Wine

A Country of Vines

Moldova has roughly 112,000 hectares under vine — an extraordinary figure for a country the size of Belgium. The continental climate, with warm summers and cold winters, suits both international varieties and indigenous grapes. The wine regions stretch from the rolling Codru hills in the centre to the warm steppe of Valul lui Traian in the south.

Indigenous Grape Varieties

Fetească Neagră — Moldova's proudest red grape. Deep garnet in colour, it produces wines of dark fruit, dried plum, and subtle spice. With age, it develops a velvety smoothness that surprises those expecting a rustic eastern-European red.

Fetească Albă — A white variety of great delicacy: pale straw, floral on the nose, with notes of peach and white blossom. Best drunk young and slightly chilled.

Rară Neagră — Rare outside Moldova. Makes lighter, brighter reds with lifted acidity and cherry-red fruit. An excellent partner for sarmale.

The Underground Cellars

The Mileștii Mici winery near Chișinău holds the Guinness World Record for the largest wine cellar by number of bottles — over 1.5 million, stored in 55 kilometres of underground limestone galleries. Visitors tour the galleries by bicycle or car. Cricova, another famous cellar, has hosted heads of state and stored wine at a constant 12 °C since the 1950s.

Spirits

Divin (Moldovan Brandy)

Moldova's answer to cognac. Distilled from local white wines and aged in oak barrels, divin ranges from three-year blends — smooth and vanilla-forward — to twenty-year reserves of amber complexity. KVINT, based in Tiraspol, has been producing divin since 1897.

Rachiu (Fruit Brandy)

Every village family with a fruit tree makes rachiu. Plums are most common (rachiu de prune), but apricot, pear, grape pomace (tescovină), and even quince versions exist. Home-distilled, clear, and fierce — offered to guests as soon as they sit down, whether it is 10 a.m. or 10 p.m.

Non-Alcoholic Drinks

Borș (Fermented Wheat Brine)

Not a drink in the conventional sense, but sipped warm as a remedy and used in cooking as a souring agent. Wheat bran is fermented in warm water for two to three days; the resulting liquid is tart, slightly funky, and strangely refreshing.

Compot (Stewed Fruit Drink)

Cherries, apricots, or plums are simmered with sugar and water, then cooled and served over ice in summer or warm in winter. Every household jars compot at harvest time; opening a jar in January tastes like a preserved summer afternoon.

Kefir & Lapte Bătut

Cultured dairy drinks are everyday staples. Lapte bătut (literally "beaten milk") is a thin, tangy yoghurt drunk from a glass. It accompanies plăcinte, cools spicy stews, and is considered a tonic for the digestive system.

Pairing

Wine with Sarmale

A semi-dry Rară Neagră or Fetească Neagră cuts through the richness of pork and pickled cabbage beautifully.

Pairing

Divin with Dessert

An aged divin alongside cozonac or walnut cake is Moldova's equivalent of port and stilton — deeply satisfying.

Pairing

Compot at the Table

Cold cherry compot alongside a bowl of zeamă is a lunch combination found in every traditional Moldovan household.
